Modern banking is dependent on a complex web of supply chains, talent pools, infrastructure and technologies. The ability to prevent, respond to, recover and learn from operational disruptions to survive and prosper is essential for any organisation. The increasing complexity in critical processes provides a perfect environment for serious operational vulnerabilities to hide - until they make themselves visible through a catastrophic crisis. Therefore, OR requires a fundamental change of mindset from preventing to managing. Precautionary measures are undoubtedly important, but at the same time, what we should be emphasising more is the ability to manage the impact when something damaging has already happened.  
“Imagine you have a ship, you know it is certified and it is safe, but you still need a life boat and a life vest. The same logic applies to financial service. "
                                        Henry Irving (Principal, Hansuke Consulting)

A long journey ahead, a joint effort neede

Visualising systemic risks and assessing impact tolerance has never been easy. Joint efforts from all players in the industry are needed to work on OR. At the behest of the Financial Conduct Authority, Hansuke is hosting a working party for UK-based overseas and challenger banks to inform the proportionate and internationally coherent development of UK requirements for OR.  At a recent banking seminar on this very subject, FCA’s Chris Walmsley and Hansuke’s principal Henry Irving shared their latest industry insights, followed by a panel discussion with Sundeep Bhandari (Independent Non-Executive Director at Punjab National Bank), Gary Wayte (Head of Operational Risk & Resilience, One Savings Bank) and Ali Kazimi (managing director of Hansuke). 


What are the key concepts in OR?

 OR is an outcome. It is the ability to absorb shocks and maintain continuity of most important business services - assuming failure will occur. It is important for an organisation to: 

  • Identify the most important business service and how much disruption could be tolerated in what circumstances;
  • Map the system and processes that support these business services;
  • Assess how the failure of an individual system or process could impact the business service;
  • Test using severe but plausible scenario and by learning from experience, that resilience meets the firm’s tolerance;
  • Invest in ability to respond and recover from disruptions through having appropriate system, oversight and training; and,
  • Communicate timely information to internal stakeholders, supervisory authorities, customers, counterparties and other market participants
“If you are brave enough to use an ambitious approach of testing, you will find more problems, and you will create more opportunities to fix and advance operational resilience."
                                                                                    Chris Walmsley (FCA)

PSD2 Compliance: Operational and Security Risks of Payment Services


Payment service, one of the topics discussed in the seminar, is a key business service for most banks. Setting, monitoring, testing and approving risk appetite and impact tolerances in this sector are therefore vital. To achieve this, planning to respond to events effectively as well as prevent them is critical.  

Technical standards under PSD2 will come into effect on 14 September 2019, and some of the requirements need to be met before that date. Firms should be making changes to meet the requirements like strong customer authentication, reporting and notifications. 

To comply with PSD2, firms need to: 

  1. Provide security policies and procedures, including a risk assessment in relation to your payment services, and describe security controls and mitigation measures designed to protect payment service users;
  2.  Demonstrate that they have effective processes to monitor and handle incidents and security-related customer complaints;
  3. Explain how they will deal with significant continuity disruptions, such as the failure of key systems, the loss of key data, or lack of access to premises; and
  4. Demonstrate that they have an effective process to file, monitor, track, and restrict access to sensitive payment data such as strong customer authentication, data classification, access management, and monitoring tools

Firms are also responsible for the activities of their external partners and need to consider outsourcing arrangements, and they are expected to comply with open banking requirements to ensure information sharing is consistent and secure.
